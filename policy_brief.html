Rethinking Accountability in Kazakhstan's Local Governance: Lessons from Maslikhat Deputies

Drawing on original field research with Maslikhat deputies in Kokshetau, this brief argues that effective accountability reform must go beyond formal mechanisms — recognising how deputies' personal values, local identity, and institutional constraints shape governance in practice.

EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

The issue

Kazakhstan's Maslikhat deputies are among the country's most publicly accountable officials — directly elected by citizens to represent local constituencies and oversee budgets worth billions. Yet public trust in them remains low, their roles are widely misunderstood, and formal accountability mechanisms consistently fall short in practice. This brief presents findings from original qualitative research with deputies in Kokshetau city and offers five evidence-based recommendations for reform.

Despite a decade of governance reform under President Tokayev — including the 2021 Concept of Development of Local Self-Government, the 2023 introduction of a mixed electoral system for Maslikhats, and the first direct elections of district akims — the gap between formal accountability requirements and actual practice remains wide.

"Reports of Maslikhat deputies to voters are often formal in nature."

— President Kassym-Jomart Tokayev, Second Republican Forum of Maslikhat Deputies, October 2024

This presidential critique — delivered directly to over 2,300 deputies — underscores a systemic failure that this research helps explain: accountability in Kazakhstan's local governance is shaped less by formal rules than by deputies' personal values, local relationships, and the institutional constraints they navigate daily.

RESEARCH FINDINGS

What the research found

Through semi-structured interviews with Maslikhat deputies in Kokshetau, this study identified three distinct ways deputies understand accountability — and the factors that shape their behaviour in practice.

FINDING 01
Three accountability archetypes

Deputies fall into three groups: those who prioritise continuous accessibility (24/7 availability via phone and social media); those focused on delivering results while maintaining operational discretion; and those driven by civic duty rather than professional obligation.

FINDING 02
Informal mechanisms dominate

Deputies have created their own accountability channels — social media updates, direct constituent hotlines, community visits — to compensate for weak formal mechanisms. These informal systems are more trusted by citizens than official reporting processes.

FINDING 03
Institutional constraints are critical

Deputies' accountability is severely constrained by limited institutional authority relative to Akimats, the voluntary nature of their role, lack of resources, and low public awareness of what Maslikhat deputies can actually do.

A key theoretical contribution of this research is the application of felt accountability theory to a post-Soviet context. Deputies' sense of accountability is driven primarily by personal reputation, community connection, and constituent need — not by formal institutional pressures. This "felt" accountability, rooted in personal values rather than rules, is both a strength and a vulnerability of the current system.

POLICY RECOMMENDATIONS

Five recommendations for reform

These recommendations are designed to be realistic within Kazakhstan's current political context — they work with existing reform momentum rather than requiring systemic democratic transformation.

1
SHORT TERM

Standardise constituent reporting — but make it substantive

Mandatory reporting sessions already exist but are widely acknowledged as performative. Reform should require deputies to publish structured reports — covering budget decisions, constituency requests received, requests resolved, and requests pending — on a public digital platform. This converts reporting from a ritual into a transparency tool, directly addressing President Tokayev's October 2024 critique.

2
SHORT TERM

Invest in public education about Maslikhat roles

A recurring theme in this research is that citizens do not understand what Maslikhat deputies can and cannot do — leading to unrealistic expectations and frustration. A targeted public information campaign, delivered through schools, local media, and social platforms, would build the civic literacy needed for accountability to function. UNDP Kazakhstan's ongoing local governance support programme offers a ready vehicle for this.

3
MEDIUM TERM

Formalise and resource deputies' informal accountability mechanisms

Deputies have already built effective accountability tools — constituency hotlines, social media channels, community visits — using personal resources. Government should formally recognise these mechanisms, provide modest administrative support, and incorporate them into deputies' formal accountability frameworks. This legitimises what already works rather than imposing new bureaucratic requirements.

4
MEDIUM TERM

Rebalance institutional authority between Maslikhats and Akimats

Deputies consistently report that their ability to hold local executive bodies accountable is severely constrained by power imbalances with Akimats. Strengthening Maslikhats' oversight powers — including clearer mechanisms for questioning akim decisions and reviewing budget implementation — would give formal accountability teeth. This aligns with the direction of Tokayev's 2022–2024 reform agenda.

5
LONG TERM

Extend this research to other regions and include citizen perspectives

This study focused on Kokshetau — a mid-sized city whose dynamics may differ significantly from rural districts, smaller towns, or major urban centres like Almaty and Astana. A comparative multi-region study incorporating both deputy and citizen perspectives would provide the evidence base needed to design nationally scalable reform. International partners including UNDP, OECD, and the World Bank have all signalled interest in supporting Kazakhstan's local governance reform agenda.

CONCLUSION

The bottom line

Accountability in Kazakhstan's local governance cannot be fixed by formal mechanisms alone. This research shows that deputies' sense of responsibility is deeply personal — driven by reputation, community belonging, and civic values — and that effective reform must work with these motivations rather than against them.

The good news is that genuine reform momentum exists. President Tokayev's rhetorical commitment to accountability, the 2023 electoral reforms, and the first direct akim elections all signal political will. What is needed now is a shift from formal compliance to substantive engagement — and from top-down mandates to reforms that reflect how accountability actually works on the ground.

Kazakhstan's local governance reform offers lessons not only for the country itself but for other post-Soviet states navigating the same transition: hybrid regimes can develop authentic accountability cultures, but only when reform is grounded in the lived experience of the officials it seeks to change.
